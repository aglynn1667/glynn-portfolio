Pyoderma gangrenosum

An uncommon neutrophilic dermatosis.

  • Presents as an inflammatory and ulcerative disorder of the skin

  • Does not just affect one part of the body

  • Not gangrenous or infectious

Most common presentation:

  • Inflammatory papule or pustule

  • Progresses to painful ulcer

  • Often has a purulent base

May also present with bullous, vegetative, peristomal, and extracutaneous lesions.

More than 50% of patients with P. gangrenosum have associated underlying systemic disease. Most common/most frequently represented comorbidities:

  • Inflammatory bowel disease

  • Hematologic disorders

  • Arthritis

Etiology

Rare disorder:

  • 3–10 cases per million people per year

  • Average age 40–60 years

  • Affects females more commonly than males

Theories for development lean toward:

  • Abnormalities in neutrophil function

  • Genetic variations

  • Dysregulation of the innate immune system

Clinical Course

Characterized by the appearance of an inflammatory papule, pustule, vesicle or nodule that expands and breaks down to form an erosion or ulcer.

  • Ulcerative (most common)

  • Bullous (atypical)

  • Pustular

  • Vegetative

Diagnosis

History and physical are critical.

  • Consistent clinical and histologic findings are critical to proper diagnosis.

  • Neutrophil-predominant infiltrates

  • Exclusion of other inflammatory or ulcerative cutaneous disorders
