Cellulitis Syndromes

Post–vascular Surgery Cellulitis

Etiology

The most significant aspect of post-surgical infections is that many patients have vascular grafts placed.

Microorganisms

Most common:

  • Coag-negative Staphylococcus
  • Staphylococcus aureus

Others:

  • Non-tuberculosis mycobacteria
  • Nocardia species
  • Legionella species
  • Mycoplasma species
  • Cutibacterium species

Inguinal

Epidemiology

Abdominal aortic aneurysm repair and endovascular techniques are associated with the lowest rates of SSI (<1%).

Infrainguinal open bypass procedures show the highest rates (10–30%).

Etiology

In vascular surgery, SSTI is reported to occur in 5–10% of patients.

Gram-positive organisms are the most common pathogens, causing up to 80% of vascular SSTI. Most frequently:

  • S. Aureus
  • Coagulase-negative staphylococci

About 20–25% of vascular SSTIs are attributable to Gram-negative bacteria. Most frequently:

  • Escherichia coli
  • Pseudomonas aeruginosa
  • Proteus ssp.
  • Klebsiella pneumoniae

Antibiotic-resistant organisms are increasingly responsible for SSTI

  • Many studies now report MRSA as most common pathogenic organism responsible for SSTI

Management

Empiric parenteral therapy

Vancomycin plus cefepime or piperacillin-tazobactam

Alternative empiric parenteral therapy

Daptomycin or linezolid plus carbapenem

Definitive therapy should be pathogen-directed if possible and may require the addition of rifampin if a foreign body remains and S. aureus is identified.

Extracavitary VGIs: Recommendations for Management: Antimicrobial Therapy

Samson class I or II VGI: a trial of antimicrobial therapy with or without surgical débridement for 2 or 4 weeks is reasonable (Class IIa; Level of Evidence B).

Samson class III or IV VGI: postoperative antimicrobial therapy for 4 to 6 weeks is reasonable (Class IIa; Level of Evidence B).

After the initial therapy, a course of oral antimicrobial therapy for 6 weeks to 6 months may be considered (Class IIb; Level of Evidence B).

Samson class V VGI: postoperative antimicrobial therapy for 4 to 6 weeks administered parenterally, followed by at least 6 months of therapy administered orally may be considered (Class IIb; Level of Evidence B).

Samson class III, IV, or V VGI: long-term suppressive antimicrobial therapy may be considered for:

  • Infection caused by
    • MRSA
    • Pseudomonas
    • Multidrug-resistant organisms
    • Candida
    • Other fungal species
  • Patients who
    • Have undergone emergency or multiple surgeries
    • Have graft preservation or in situ reconstruction with extensive perigraft infection
    • Are poor candidates for reoperation

(Class IIb; Level of Evidence B).

Extracavitary VGIs: Recommendations for Management: Surgical Therapy

Samson class III infection, early (<2 months postoperatively): reasonable to consider graft preservation rather than graft excision and reconstruction (Class IIa; Level of Evidence B).

Samson class III infection (<2 months postoperatively): graft excision and reconstruction may be considered instead of graft preservation (Class IIb; Level of Evidence B).

Samson class III or lV infection caused by MRSA, Pseudomonas, or multidrug-resistant microorganisms or for patients for whom graft preservation or in situ reconstruction has failed, it is reasonable to perform extra-anatomic revascularization, followed by graft excision instead of graft preservation or in situ reconstruction (Class IIa; Level of Evidence B).

Samson class V VGI: extra-anatomic revascularization followed by graft excision is reasonable (Class IIa; Level of Evidence B).

Samson class III, IV, or V VGI: ultrasound examination every 3 to 6 months for 2 years, followed by lifelong ultrasound examination every 6 to 12 months, is reasonable (Class IIa; Level of Evidence B).

AV shunts

  • A permanent vascular access made by using an existing artery and vein

  • Used for long-term hemodialysis

Likely organism/source

  • Staphylococcus aureus

  • Staphylococcus epidermidis

  • Patient at high risk of bacteremia during access of AV fistula

Risk factors for infection

  • Pseudoaneurysm

  • Hematoma

  • Pruritus of shunt

  • Misuse of access (IV drugs)

Treatment

IV antibiotics and surgical drainage

Assess for:

  • Fever
  • Skin integrity
  • Bacteremia with blood cultures
  • Fluid culture if abscess present
  • Signs of systemic infection

Although AV fistulas SSTI may not be the source of IE, they nevertheless need to be excluded due to the proximity of endovascular infection.

Empiric parental therapy (See SSTI)

Treatment pathogen-directed or empiric for staphylococcus or streptococcus

Parenteral therapy

MSSA: Nafcillin, cefazolin, clindamycin

MRSA: Vancomycin, daptomycin linezolid, clindamycin

Oral therapy

MSSA: Clindamycin, Cephalexin, Trim-sulfa, Doxycycline

MRSA: Doxycycline, Trim-sulfa
