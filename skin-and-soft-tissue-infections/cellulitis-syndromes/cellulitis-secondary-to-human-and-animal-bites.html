Cellulitis Syndromes

Cellulitis Secondary to Human and Animal Bites

Human bite cellulitis

Etiology

The most common pathogens are viridans Streptococci, especially S. anginosus, followed by:

  • Staphylococcus aureus
  • Haemophilus influenzae
  • H. parainfluenzae
  • Aggregatibacter (Haemophilus) aphrophilus
  • Aggregatibacter (Haemophilus) paraphrophilus
  • Klebsiella spp
  • Enterobacter cloacae
  • Prevotella spp
  • Finegoldia (Peptostreptococcus) spp
  • F. nucelatum

Dog and cat bite cellulitis

Etiology

The most common pathogen is Pasteurella multocida. Other pathogens include:

  • Capnocytophaga spp
  • Streptococcus spp
  • Staphylococcus spp (including aureus)
  • Neisseria spp

Anaerobes in dog bites include:

  • Fusobacterium spp
  • Porphyromonas spp
  • Prevotella spp

Anaerobes in cat bites include:

  • Fusobacterium spp
  • Porphyromonas spp
  • Bacteroides spp

Management

Pre-emptive outpatient therapy

It is recommended to treat moderate-to-severe animal bite wounds, especially of the hands and face, prior to the onset of infectious signs and symptoms for 3–5 days.

Treatment of choice is amoxicillin-clavulanate 875-125 mg po bid.

Alternative therapies include:

  • Moxifloxacin 400mg po qd
  • Comination of:
    • Doxycycline 100 mg po bid or levofloxacin 750 mg po qd
    • Metronidazole 500 mg po tid or clindamycin 450 mg po tid

Rat bite cellulitis

Etiology

Most common pathogens:

  • Streptobacillus moniliformis
  • Streptobacillus notomytis
  • Spirillum minor

Management

Empiric parenteral therapy

Vancomycin and cefepime or piperacillin-tazobactam

Pathogen-directed parenteral therapy

Penicillin G or ceftriaxone

Pathogen-directed oral therapy

Penicillin V, Ampicillin, or Amoxacillin

Duration of therapy

Best treated with IV therapy for the first 5–7 days, followed by oral therapy to complete a 14-day course.
