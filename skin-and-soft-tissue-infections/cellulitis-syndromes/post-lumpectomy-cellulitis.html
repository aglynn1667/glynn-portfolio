Cellulitis Syndromes

Post-Lumpectomy Cellulitis

Ipsilateral breast

(Can extend to the remainder of the breast, anterior shoulder, back, and ipsilateral upper extremity)

  • Cellulitis may occur months/years after surgery

  • May be due to a delayed inflammatory process

Likely organism/source

  • Group B beta-hemolytic streptococci

  • Staphylococcus aureus (MRSA, MSSA)

Diagnosis

  • Clinical picture:

    • Pain
    • Diffuse erythema
    • Tenderness
    • Warmth
    • Swollen axillary nodes

  • Ultrasound to assess for subcutaneous edema

  • MRI: skin thickening and separation

Cellulitis management

Symptomatic relief and antibiotics.

  • NSAIDS, cold compress

  • Uncomplicated: PO cephalexin, PO clindamycin

  • Clinically worsening: systemtic signs of infection, need blood cultures, and IV antibiotics

    • Ampicillin, Amp/sulbactam, cefazolin, vancomycin, or clindamycin

    • Transition to PO when clinically improving (amox/clav)

  • Known MRSA history: treat with doxy, clindamycin, trim-sulfa, or linezolid
