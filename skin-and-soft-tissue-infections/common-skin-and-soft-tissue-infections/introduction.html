Common Skin and Soft Tissue Infections

Introduction

The result of acute, spreading pyogenic infection.

Typically involve both the epidermis and dermis.

Manifest as a localized area of erythema.

Can be classified as uncomplicated or complicated:

Uncomplicated:

  • Respond to standard antimicrobials alone or simple incision and drainage

  • Usually a healthy host

Complicated:

  • Do not respond to standard antimicrobials

  • Caused by unusual or multi-drug-resistant pathogens

  • More invasive

  • Require extensive debridement

  • Cause system signs and symptoms of infection

  • Host may or may not have significant underlying comorbid illnesses

Risk factors

  • Any alteration of normnal, intact skin.

    • Wounds

    • Ulcers

    • Dermatological conditions

  • Trauma

    • Burns

    • Crush injuries

    • Open fractures

  • Post-operative

  • Irradiation of skin

    • From sun or, e.g., during cancer treatment

  • IVDU

  • Human or animal bites

  • Maceration and breakdown from prolonged exposure to water

    • Fresh or salt water

  • Comorbid illness

    • Diabetes

    • Chronic renal failure

    • Liver failure

    • Neutropenia

    • Lymphedema or arterial insufficiency

  • Occupational or recreational exposure

    • Fishing

    • Butchers or hunters

    • Veterinarians

    • Domesticated or agricultural animal exposure

Most common Microbial causes – pyogenic (bacterial) infections

  • Staphylococcus aureus (MSSA, MRSA)

  • B-hemolytic streptococci (groups A, B, C, G)

Other pathogenic causes based on condition or exposure

  • Diabetes – often polymicrobial (S. aureus, Pseudomonas spp, and/or Bacteroides spp (anaerobes)

  • Cirrhosis – Vibrio vulnificus

  • Butcher or veterinarian – Erysipelothrix spp

  • Fisherman – Erysipelothrix spp

  • Fish tank exposure (hobby) - Mycobacterium marinum

  • Hot tub exposure – Pseudomonas spp

  • Dog or cat bites – Pasteurella multocida (See Animal Bites)

  • IVDU – MRSA or Pseudomonas spp

  • Tick bite – Borrelia burgdorferi

  • Freshwater exposure – Aeromonas spp

  • Saltwater exposure – Vibrio vulnificus

Clinical manifestations:

Variable, depending on several factors:

  • Anatomical site

  • Host comorbid illness

  • Immune response

  • Pathogen

Classic findings (can vary depending on early treatment, comorbid status of the host, immunocompromise):

  • Redness (rubor)

  • Warmth (calor)

  • Swelling (tumor)

  • Tenderness or pain (dolor)

  • Fever (may be absent in early treatment or immunocompromise)

  • Fatigue

  • Malaise

  • Arthralgias or myalgias

  • Chills (may indicate associated bacteremia)

  • Pain

    • Mild or moderate tenderness or pain is classic

    • Severe pain may indicate a necrotizing condition (emergency)

  • Purulent drainage

    • Typically associated with abscess caused by MRSA

    • Foul odor drainage may indicate anaerobic infection

    • Sweet or fruity odor may indicate Pseudomonas
