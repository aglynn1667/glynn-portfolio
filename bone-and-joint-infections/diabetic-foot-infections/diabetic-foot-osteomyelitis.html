Diabetic Foot Infections

Diabetic Foot Osteomyelitis (DFO)

Osteomyelitis in infected diabetic foot creates a high risk of below-the-knee amputation. It is a consequence of non-healing ulcer infection that spread to the bone.

It can affect any foot bone, but the forefoot is most involved (90% of cases), with midfoot in 5% and hindfoot in 5% of cases. (1)

Common pathogens

The infection is mostly polymicrobial.

  • S. aureus can be found in 50% of cases

  • Enterococci in 40%

  • Strep species in 30%

  • Staph epi in 25%

  • Among gram-negative organisms, most common (2) are:

    • E. coli

    • K. pneumoniae

    • Proteus

    • P. aeruginosa

Diagnosis

Physical exam

The depth and width of an ulcer have a high clinical importance. (3)

  • An ulcer larger than 2 cm2 has a sensitivity of 56% and a specificity of 92%

  • An ulcer deeper than 3 mm is more associated with an underlying osteomyelitis than superficial ulcers (82% vs. 33%)

Prob-to-bone test

If a sterile probe can reach the bone, it strongly suggests that the bone is already infected with a sensitivity of 66% and a specificity of 85%. (4)

Imaging

Imaging suggesting osteomyelitis.

Labs

CBC, ESR, CRP, and bone biopsy.

Avoid wound swab.

Treatment

Conservative surgery along with Abx.

  • Conservative surgery denotes a procedure in which only dead tissue is removed without performing total amputation.

Duration of Abx:

  • 4–6 weeks if the infected bone was not fully removed

  • 3 months when Abx used only
