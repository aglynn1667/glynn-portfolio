Diabetic Foot Infections

Diabetic Foot Ulcer

Introduction

Diabetic foot ulcer infections typically begin in a wound. The infection is defined by ≥2 classic findings of inflammation or purulence.

Classic signs of inflammation:

  • Redness

  • Warmth

  • Swelling

  • Tenderness

  • Pain

Purulence:

  • Purulent secretions

May also include secondary signs:

  • Non-purulent secretions

  • Friable or discolored granulation tissue

  • Undermining of wound edges

  • Foul odor

Although clinically uninfected wounds do not require antibiotic therapy, infected wounds do. (1)

Diagnosis

Imaging

X-Ray, MRI to detect bone damage and/or soft tissue involvement.

Labs

CBC, ESR, CRP, CMP.

Tissue biopsy should not be performed on a non-infected diabetic foot.

Patients with infected diabetic foot should receive a deep tissue biopsy culture and stain with anaerobe, AFB stain, and fungus culture before starting Abx.

A bone sample should be sent to pathology.

Avoid swab of inadequately debrided wounds.

Treatment

No Abx needed for non-infected diabetic foot ulcer

Empirical treatment according to severity for diabetic foot ulcer infection:

  • Mild to moderate infected diabetic foot ulcer: cover for gram-positive cocci (GPC), such as streptococcus bacteria.

  • Severely infected diabetic foot ulcers should be treated empircally with vancomycin to cover MRSA.

Empirical covering of Pseudomonas aeruginosa is not indicated unless the patient has risk factors (e.g., a previous wound infection with P.Â aeruginosa).

Empirical covering for MRSA should be given for patients with risk factors, such as h/o MRSA previous infections. (1)

Hospitalization

Recommended for:

  • All patients with a severe infection

  • Selected patients with a moderate infection with complicated features, such as:

    • Severe peripheral arterial disease (PAD)

    • Lack of home support
