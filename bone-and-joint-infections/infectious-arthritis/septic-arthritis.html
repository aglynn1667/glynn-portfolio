Septic Arthritis

Epidemiology

Native joint septic arthritis has an annual incidence of 2-12 per 100,000 people per year.

Risk factors include:

  • Advanced age (>65 years old)

  • Concomitant rheumatoid arthritis (28-70 per 100,000 people per year)

  • Anti-TNFα therapy

  • Lower socio-economic status.

Pathophysiology

Synovium is susceptible to hematogenous seeding because it is vascularized but lacks a basement membrane.

Gram-positive organisms bind connective tissue and extracellular matrix proteins better than Gram-negative organisms.

Cartilage, which is avascular and dependent on the synovium for oxygen and nutrients, is damaged by:

  • Bacterial enzymes and toxins

  • White blood cell lysosomal proteases and reactive oxygen species

Routes of infection include:

  • Adjacent skin and soft tissue injury (skin flora)

  • Urosepsis (urogenital flora)

  • Bacterial translocation (gastrointestinal flora)

  • IV drug use

  • Direct innoculation secondary to:

    • Surgery

    • Penetrating trauma

    • Human or animal bites

Etiology

S. aureus is the most common bacterial pathogen (42% MSSA and 10% MRSA), followed by:

  • Coagulase-negative Staphylococcus spp.

  • Streptococci spp.

  • Gram-negative bacilli

  • M. tuberculosis

  • N. gonorrhoeae

  • Anaerobes

  • Borrelia burgdorferi

Among the coagulase-negative Staphylococcus spp, S. lugdunensis is especially virulent.

GBS often causes infection in neonates and the elderly, especially those with:

  • Diabetes

  • Cirrhosis

  • Malignancy

  • Genitourinary structural abnormalities

  • Neurologic disease

Pneumococcus formerly compromised 6% of cases but more recently was found to account for only 3% of cases, most likely due to more widespread immunization.

Gonococcus now accounts for 1–3% of cases but is becoming more common due to emerging fluoroquinolone resistance. Disseminated gonococcal infection results in either of:

  • Septic arthritis

  • Triad of dermatitis, tenosynovitis, and polyarthralgia

Disseminated gonococcal infection is:

  • Usually diagnosed in people >40 years old

  • 4 times more common in women than in men

Presentation

Joint pain, swelling, warmth, and restricted movement occur in 80% of patients with septic arthritis.

  • Majority also present with fever

  • Elderly may be afebrile

Non-gonococcal arthritis is monarthric in 80–90% of cases and affects the knee in 50%.

The wrists, ankles, and hips are other commonly affected joints.

Involvement of the small joints of the hands and feet and of the sternoclavicular or sternomanubrial joint suggests IV drug use.

Polyarthric septic arthritis is more common in the setting of sepsis or connective tissue disease, such as rheumatoid arthritis or systemic Lupus erythematosus.

Diagnosis

Labs: CBC, CMP, ESR, CRP, synovial fluid cell counts, and GC/CT NAATs

Imaging: US vs CT vs. MRI (helpful to diagnosis periarthric osteomyelitis)

Microbiology: aerobic, anaerobic, fungal, and AFB synovial fluid cultures
