Orthopedic Device Infection

Prosthetic Joint Infections

Prosthetic joints

Definitive

  • Presence of a sinus tract, which is directly communicating to the prosthesis.

  • Evidence of purulence surrounding the prosthesis without contiguous site of infection.

  • Two or more intra-operative positive cultures that yield the same organism.

  • Growth of Staphylococcus aureus in a single culture tissue or synovial fluid.

Suggestive

  • Evidence of acute inflammation of the peri-prosthetic tissue.

Treatment of PJI following debridement and retention of prosthesis (see Table 2).

PJI Due to Staphylococcus infection

2–6 weeks of pathogen-directed IV antibiotics in combination with Rifampin 600 mg PO QD followed by Rifampin, plus a companion oral drug (Ciprofloxacin or Levofloxacin).

  • Guidelines indicate using 300–450 mg PO QD

Secondary companion drugs include:

  • Doxycycline

  • Cephalexin

  • Dicloxacillin

If Rifampin cannot be used due to allergy or intolerance, proceed with 4–6 weeks of pathogen-directed IV therapy.

Chronic Suppressive Therapy

  • Cephalexin, Dicloxacillin, Minocycline based on in vitro susceptibilities.

  • Rifampin alone, or combination therapy is generally not recommended for chronic suppressive therapy.

For suppressive therapy (Oral): see Table 2.

PJI secondary to other pathogens

4–6 weeks of pathogen-directed IV or highly bioavailable oral

Management

Medical management of PJI following resection arthroplasty with or without planned staged reimplantation

4–6 weeks of pathogen-specific IV or highly bioavailable oral antimicrobial therapy

Monitoring of outpatient intravenous antimicrobial therapy with inflammatory markers (CRP, ESR) and CMP and CBC.

Medical management for a patient with PJI following 1-stage exchange?

Less commonly preferred in the U.S. compared to Europe.

Medical management for a patient with PJI following 2-stage exchange?

Management of MSSA/MSSE and/or MRSA/MRSE: IV or PO regimen for 4–6 weeks post explantation of infected device.

Antimicrobials should have activity against infecting organisms.

Medical treatment for a patient with PJI following amputation:

If evidence of residual infected bone and soft tissue (THA Infection, TKA prosthesis above level of amputation), plan for 4-6 weeks of pathogen-specific IV or highly bioavailable oral antimicrobial therapy.

If after amputation, all infected bone and soft tissue has been surgically removed and it has been ascertained that there is no concomitant sepsis or bacteremia, treat with pathogen-directed therapy for 24-48 hours after amputation.

Recent guidelines for the treatment of methicillin-resistant Staphylococcus aureus (MRSA) infections have been published. (These guidelines suggest that dosing of vancomycin be considered to achieve a vancomycin trough at steady state of 15 to 20. Although this may be appropriate for MRSA PJI treated without rifampin or without the use of local vancomycin spacer, it is unknown if these higher trough concentrations are necessary when rifampin or vancomcyin impregnated spacers are utilized.
