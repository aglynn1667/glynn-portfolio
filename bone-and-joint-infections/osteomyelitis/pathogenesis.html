Osteomyelitis

Pathogenesis

Two major mechanisms of osteomyelitis are contiguous and hematogenous.

Contiguous

  • When the integrity of the cortex is breached.

  • When microorganisms contaminate directly through an adjacent ulcer, trauma, or surgery on the bone.

  • When microbial contamination causes progressive destruction of the bone.

  • When diabetic foot ulcers, open fractures, or surgical procedures contribute contiguous inoculation.

Hematogenous

  • Hematogenous injection always seeds somewhere else from the body.

  • e.g., SSTI blood stream infection ⇛ epidural abscess ⇛ native vertebral osteomyelitis

  • This form of infection is quite common in pediatric age children with acute long bone osteomyelitis.

  • Implications of hematogenous Osteomyelitis:

    • Risk factors include:

      • Endocarditis

      • Injection drug use

      • Sickle cell disease

      • Hemodialysis

      • Presence of cardiovascular devices

      • Indwelling intravascular devices or orthopedic hardware

    • Most common site of Osteomyelitis for people who inject drugs (PWID) involves the vertebral column, especially lumbar spine. However, it can also occur in the extremities, sacroiliac joint, and sternum.

    • Of particular interest in this subgroup of PWID is their predilection to develop osteomyelitis from oral flora pathogens through needle licking prior to injection.

Emphysematous Osteomyeletis

  • This is a rare form of osteomyelitis, primarily affecting the pelvis, sacrum, lower extremity, or vertebrae.

  • It can extend either hematogenously or contiguously via an intra-abdominal source, such as complications from GI malignancy.

  • Most common pathogens are Enterobacteriaceae and fusobacterium.
