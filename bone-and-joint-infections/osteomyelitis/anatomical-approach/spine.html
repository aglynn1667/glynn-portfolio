Osteomyelitis

Anatomical Approach

Spine

Native Vertebral Osteomyeletis

Etiology

Occurs mainly due to hematogenous seeding of disc from a distant focus.

Microorganisms

Most common cause is Staphylococcus aureus. Other causes are enteric gram-negative bacilli, viridans streptococci-milleri group. streptococcus gallolyticus (bovis), enterococci, group B and group C streptococcus, Pseudomonas aeruginosa, coag negative staph.

Candida species are seen in association with intravascular access, sepsis, or IV drug use, and esophageal performation.

Tuberculosis, Brucellosis

Clinical Features

  • Back/neck pain that is worsened on movement and physical activity. Can radiate to abdomen, groin, perineum, arms.

  • The pain can be associated with radicular symptoms, including numbness, tingling, paresthesias.

  • Sometimes, it can lead to loss of bowel and bladder control, loss of perineal sensation, and paralysis.

  • Fever.

Diagnosis

  • Appropriate history, such as IV drug use, recent bacteremia, recent dental work, trauma, previous vertebral osteomyelitis with neurological exam example decreased sensations, decreased sphincter tone, hyperreflexia.

  • Baseline ESR and CRP-Increased ESR; CRP is not specific but has a sensitivity of 94–100%.

  • MRI spine.

  • If MRI of the spine is not possible, recommend radionucleotide bone scan, CT scan, or positron emission tomography scan.

  • Blood cultures.

  • Patients from endemic areas (e.g., from Mediterranean basin)—Brucellosis serology, PPD, or interferon gamma release assay for patients at risk.

  • Image-guided biopsy is recommended when a microbiologic diagnosis for a known associated organism has not been established by blood cultures or serologic tests.

Treatment

  • Empiric therapy should be deferred until an image-guided aspiration and/or biopsy of the affected area is performed.

  • Empiric therapy should not be deferred in life-threatening conditions, such as sepsis or impending spinal cord or nerve root compression (in these situations, may require adjective steroids).

  • Empiric therapy directed against methicillin-resistant S. aureus(MRSA), streptococci, and gram-negative organisms, e.g. IV vancomycin and third or fourth generation cephalosporin/ ciprofloxacin/ carbapenem. Alternative regimen: combination of daptomycin and a quinolone.

Duration of therapy:

  • Total duration of 6 weeks of parenteral or highly bioavailable oral antimicrobial therapy for most patients with bacterial NVO.

  • Total duration of 3 months of antimicrobial therapy for most patients with NVO due to Brucella species.

When to do surgery:

  • Progressive neurologic deficits, progressive deformity, and spinal instability with or without pain despite adequate antimicrobial therapy.

  • Patients with persistent or recurrent bloodstream infection (without alternative source) or worsening pain despite appropriate medical therapy.

  • Large epidural abscess.

  • No need surgical debridement and/or stabilization in patients who have worsening bony imaging findings at 4–6 weeks in the setting of improvement in clinical symptoms, physical examination, and inflammatory markers.

Follow-up therapy:

  • Persistent pain, residual neurologic deficits, elevated markers of systemic inflammation, or radiographic findings alone do not necessarily signify treatment failure in treated NVO patients.

  • The most specific measure of treatment failure is microbiologically confirmed persistent infection despite receipt of targeted antimicrobial therapy for an appropriate duration.

  • Monitoring systemic inflammatory markers (ESR and or CRP) after approximately 4 weeks of antimicrobial therapy, in conjunction with a clinical assessment.

Spine osteomyelitis with hardware

Etiology

Vertebral osteomyelitis in the presence of spinal hardware occurs usually in the presence of wound infection rather than hematogenous seeding.

Microorganisms

Most common pathogen is S. aureus (MRSA, MSSA), followed by coag-negative staph. Others: gram negative organisms and anaerobes.

Clinical signs

Fever, worsening back pain, neuropathic pain. Severe symptoms are stool/urine incontinence, paralysis, or even death.

Antibiotic and surgical management

Decision regarding surgical management should be made by the orthopedic surgeon with appropriate consultation.
