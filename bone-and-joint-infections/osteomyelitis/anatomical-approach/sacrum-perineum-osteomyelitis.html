Pubis Osteomyelitis

Risk factors:

  • IV drug use

  • Cardiac catheterization

  • Bacteremia

  • Pelvic surgery

  • Urologic surgery

  • Trauma

Secondary to trauma/surgery, infection from soft tissue (pressure ulcer), septic arthritis, rarely spontaneous infection, hematogenous (rare).

Diagnosis

  • Bone biopsy

  • MRI

  • Positive fluid culture from sinus tract to pelvic bone

Bacteria/pathogens

  • Hematogenous: S. aureus most common, but any bacteria possible

  • IV drug use: Pseudomonas aeruginosa

  • Post-operative: secondary to decubitus ulcer, polymicrobial or containing GI/GU pathogens

Management

  • Surgical debridement I&D washout of infection if possible

  • Long-term IV antibiotics of 6–8 weeks

  • Culture (-) treatment:

    • Empiric therapy to cover gram (+) staph/strep, gram (-) and anaerobic organisms from GI/GU tract

    • Vancomycin + pip/tazo or 3rd/4th gen cephalosporin (cefepime, ceftriaxone) + metronidazole

    • Duration of therapy: 6 weeks minimum

  • Culture (+) treatment:

    • In gram-negative organisms: fluoroquinolones (ciprofloacin, levofloxacin) or ceftriaxone, cefepime

    • Cutibacterium: ceftriaxone

    • Staph: MSSA: nafcillin, cefazolin, ceftriaxone

    • Staph: MRSA: vancomycin, daptomycin

    • Ampicillin-resistant Enterococci: vancomycin, daptomycin

    • Vancomycin-resistant Enterococci: daptomycin, linezolid (not more than 2 weeks)

    • Anaerobes: pip/tazo or metronidazole

  • Presence of retained hardware will need treatment with rifampin when gram-positive cocci organism is present
