Anatomical Approach

Head and Neck

Skull Base Osteomyeletis

Introduction

Skull base osteomyelitis is a complication of otitis externa and media, as well as infections of the mastoid and sinuses. If due to ear infections, patients will present with severe otalgia and unilateral otorrhea. If there is extension of the local infection, lower cranial nerve palsies ensue, especially involvement of the facial nerve, which is present in 60% of patients. Severe cases can also present as bilateral skull base osteomyelitis and bilateral cranial nerve palsies. (1)

Pathogens

The most common pathogen for skull base osteomyelitis. P. Aeruginosa. Followed by Staphylococcus aureus.

Diagnosis

Imaging: MRI brain that shows boney destruction and inflammatory changes in the surrounding structures. (Figure 1).

Labs: CBC with diff. ESR, CRP.

Bone cultures: The golden standard diagnostic procedure which helps to direct the Abx toward the offensive organism. The lab order should include bone/ biopsy culture and stain, bone/ biopsy anaerobic culture, bone/ biopsy AFB culture and stain, bone/ biopsy fungus culture.

Treatment

Same as in other types of osteomyelitis. Necrotic tissue should be debrided until reaching a clear tissue border. Empirical Abx with Vancomycin to cover MRSA plus piperacillin-tazobactam to cover P. aeruginosa and other gram-negative bacteria and anaerobes pending the bone culture.

Duration of treatment: 6–12 weeks.

Mandibular Osteomyelitis

Introduction

Mandibular osteomyelitis can be due to chronic periapical infection or due to blood stream infection. Osteonecrosis with bisphosphonate use or radiation therapy.

Common pathogens

Viridians streptococci and other streptococci, Staphylococcus spp., Finegoldia (formerly known as Peptostreptococcus spp.), Bacteroides spp., and other oral anaerobes.

Signs

  • Tenderness

  • Trismus

  • Halitosis

  • Soft tissue swelling

  • Sinus drainage

  • Lymphadenopathy

  • The mandible is more commonly involved than the maxilla

Symptoms

  • Painful mastication

  • Fever

  • Swollen gingiva

  • Lower lip numbness. (2)

Diagnosis

Physical exam

Imaging: X-ray, CT maxillofacial, MRI.

Labs: CBC, ESR, CRP, CMP, bone biopsy culture and stain, anaerobe culture, AFB stain, fungus culture and pathology.

Treatment: Remove the source of infection (hardwares, abscess drainage).

Abx: Empiric therapy—Clindamycin, 600 mg IV q6h, or Moxifloxacin, 400 mg PO or IV q24h. (3)

If hardware retained, rifampin should be added to prevent hardware infection. Then the patient may be discharged on suppressive therapy.
