Osteomyelitis

Anatomical Approach

Upper Extremities

Most common causes:

  • Contiguous extension of a focal source of infections related to traumatic injury

  • Acute septic arthritis of the joint

  • Pathological fractures

Empiric antimicrobial management after bone cultures are obtained by the following means.

For contiguous osteomyelitis:

  • Vancomycin plus Piperacillin-Tazobactam

  • (If there is no concern for anaerobic infection Vancomycin and cefepime is an acceptable regimen)

For hematogenous osteomyelitis:

  • Vancomycin plus (ceftriaxone, ceftazidime or cefepime).

  • Acceptable regimens include Vancomycin plus Piperacillin-Tazobactam.

Empiric antibiotics should always be based on appropriate clinical context of patient’s co-morbid conditions and historical microbiological data.

For example, an immunocompromised patient who is a gardener, pathogens such as fungal and atypical mycobacterium, fusobacterium should be considered in the differential.

Deescalate all empiric antibiotic therapy as mentioned above based on culture results and susceptibility tests.

Selected pathogens for contiguous osteomyelitis are:

  • Staphylococcus aureus

  • Coagulase-negative staphylococci

  • Gram-negative bacilli

  • Streptococcus species

  • Pseudomonas aeruginosa spp.

Selected pathogens for hematogenous osteomyelitis are:

  • Staphylococcus aureus

  • Group A Streptococcus

  • Enteric gram-negative bacilli

  • Salmonella species (especially among patients with sickle cell disease)

  • Kingella kingae (especially children under 4 years of age)

Pathogen-directed therapy (see Table 1):

  • MSSA—Nafcillin or Cefazolin

  • MRSA—Vancomycin

  • Gram-negative bacilli—Ciprofloxacin or Levofloxacin

  • Streptococcus spp.—Penicillin or Ceftriaxone

  • Pseudomonas (if susceptible)—Ciprofloxacin or Levofloxacin

  • Salmonella spp.—Ceftriaxone

  • Kingella kingae—Ceftriaxone plus anti-staphylococcal agent (defer this to pediatric section)

Humerus Osteomyeletis

Most common cause is related to either of:

  • Septic arthritis with or without blood stream infection.

  • Post-traumatic injury complications.

With retained hardware

Therapeutic approach will be pathogen-directed (see above) for six weeks, followed by suppressive therapy (see Table).

Without retained hardware

Pathogen-directed (see above) therapy for six weeks. No suppressive therapy needed.

Forearm Osteomyelitis

Same as humerus osteomyelitis.

Duration of therapy

Six to eight weeks—longer if hardware is retained.

  • Early hardware infection <4 weeks:

    • If hardware removed, treat for six weeks.

    • If hardware retained, treat until bony fusion or hardware removal.

  • Late infection >4 weeks:

    • Remove hardware if possible and treat for 6 weeks.

    • Retained hardware, treat for 3–6 months or until hardware removed.
