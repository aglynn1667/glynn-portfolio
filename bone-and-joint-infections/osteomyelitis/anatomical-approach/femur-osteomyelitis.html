Osteomyelitis

Anatomical Approach

Femur Osteomyelitis

  • Periosteum, medullary cavity, coritcal bone.

  • Acute (days to weeks) vs. chronic (months or years)

Etiology

  • Traumatic injury, penetrating injury, orthopedic surgery (contiguous spread).

  • Acute infections originating elsewhere in the body.

  • Hematogenous spread (in children and elderly tend to be more in long bones, ie tibia or femur) and in adults tend to be more vertebral (due to vascular supply).

  • In long bones, bacteria travel through and get stuck in the small vasculature that is near the epiphyses.

  • Clinical manifestations of hematogenous osteomyelitis in the long bones include chills, fever, malaise reflecting the bacteremic spread of microorganisms.

  • Pain and local swelling subsequently develop at the site of local infection.

  • Fever may not be common in chronic osteomyelitis.

Diagnostic tools/imaging/labs

Standard x-ray/plain film

  • Require 2–3 weeks to become positive (50% loss of bone calcium required).

  • Show demineralization within 2–3 weeks of infection onset.

  • In long bone infections, periosteal elevation may develop in addition to areas of reduced calcium (lytic lesions), and soft tissue swelling is apparent.

CT Scan

  • More sensitive than plain films.

  • Help define the extent of bone damage.

  • If surgical debridement is necessary, CT can help to decide the extent of debridement needed.

MRI

  • Useful to help detect early change and sequestra.

  • A unique MRI signal is noted when bone marrow dies (as a result of long bone necrosis). This helps with surgical resection and debridement process as well.

  • More sensitive than CT in detecting early osteomyelitis.

Tissue sample for culture and histopathology

  • May not be of value if blood cultures are positive (i.e., organism is known).

  • Helpful diagnostic tool in long bone infections in adults.

  • ESR/CRP/WBC (WBC and ESR elevate faster than CRP does).

Common bacteria

Hematogenous spread

Children and adults >16:

  • S. aureus

  • Coagulase-negative staphylococci

  • Gram-negative organisms such as E. coli

  • Pseudomonas spp.

  • Serrata spp.

Contiguous spread

Microbiology depends on the primary site of infection (and often polymicrobial):

  • S. aureus

  • Streptococcus pyogenes

  • Enterococcus

  • Coagulase-negative staphylococci

  • Gram-negative organisms

  • Anaerobes

Management

  • Surgical debridement

  • Antimicrobial therapy

  • Empiric therapy aimed at the clinically suspected pathogen or pathogens

  • De-escalate or change coverage once susceptibility is known

  • At least 6 weeks of antimicrobial therapy

    • If pt has retained orthopedic hardware at the site of the infection, and certain bacteria are the causative agent (ie staph spp), then Rifampin may be added (pending review for interactions with other medications) during the treatment phase.

    • Following completion of treatment dosing, suppressive therapy is added and monitored ongoing. There are no clear guidelines on the duration of suppressive therapy

  • Start date is either the day on which effective antimicrobial therapy, as judged by date of in vitro suspectibility, was begun, or on the date of the last major debridement.
