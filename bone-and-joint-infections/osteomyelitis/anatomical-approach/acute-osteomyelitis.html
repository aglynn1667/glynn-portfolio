Acute Osteomyelitis

Post-traumatic

Epidemiology

Per Waldvogel et al. (1970): (1)

  • 47%*** of osteomyelitis is post-traumatic

  • 34% stems from vascular insufficiency

  • 19% is hematogenous.

The risk of post-traumatic osteomyelitis following an open fracture ranges from 2% to 50% depending on:

  • Extent of the associated soft tissue (vascular and neurologic) injury;

  • Degree of contamination with bacteria, mycobacteria, and fungi;

  • Adequacy of surgical irrigation and debridement; and

  • Timely and appropriate administration of antibiotics.

Pathophysiology

  • Fracture violates the periosteum and exposes the underlying bone.

  • Trauma can result in the direct inoculation of pathogens into the bone.

Etiology

Most common pathogens:

  • Staphylococcus aureus

  • Coagulase-negative Staphylococci

  • Aerobic Gram-negative bacilli

Less common pathogens:

  • Enterococci

  • Anaerobes

  • Mycobacteria (both TB and NTMB)

  • Fungi, including:

    • Scedosporium apiospernum (Pseudallescheria boydii)

    • Lomentospora (Scedosporium) prolificans

    • Fusarium spp.

If the fracture was exposed to water, infection may be due to:

  • Pseudomonas

  • Aeromonas

  • Vibrio spp.

Diagnosis

Labs: CBC, CMP, ESR, and CRP

Imaging: XR vs CT vs. MRI (gold standard)

Microbiology: aerobic, anaerobic, fungal, and AFB bone cultures with or without next generation sequencing

Pathology: bone biopsy

Management

Surgical treatment is typically required, followed by appropriate antimicrobial therapy based on culture and sensitivity data.

Empiric treatment with vancomycin plus either cefepime or piperacillin-tazobactam is reasonable, pending these data.

Rifampin may need to be added to the treatment regimen if the infection is due to S. aureus and the patient has retained orthopedic hardware.

Hematogenous

Epidemiology

Osteomyelitis due to hematogenous seeding of the bone occurs mainly in:

  • Prepubertal children

  • The elderly

  • IV drug abusers

  • Patients with indwelling central venous catheters

It primarily affects the long bones but can occur—albeit rarely—in any bone in the body.

Pathophysiology

Minor trauma can produce a small hematoma followed by vascular obstruction and bone necrosis.

If this area is seeded by a transient bacteremia, osteomyelitis may ensue.

Etiology

Neonates:

  • GBS and E. coli are the most common pathogens, since they are the most common causes of neonatal sepsis.

Prepubertal Children:

  • S. aureus, Kingella kingae, and GAS are the most common pathogens.

  • H. influenzae type B and S. pneumoniae are becoming less common causes due to widespread vaccination.

IV Drug Users:

  • S. aureus, P. aeruginosa, and Candida spp are the most common pathogens.

  • Eikenella corrodens can infect those who lick their needles.

Patients with Colon Cancer (Often Undiagnosed):

  • S. gallolyticus is the most common pathogen.

  • S. lutetiensis is far less common.

Patients with Sickle Cell Disease:

  • Salmonella spp. and S. aureus are the most common pathogens.

Diagnosis

Labs: CBC, CMP, ESR, and CRP

Imaging: XR vs CT vs. MRI (gold standard)

Microbiology: aerobic, anaerobic, fungal, and AFB bone cultures with or without next generation sequencing

Pathology: bone biopsy

Management

For children, antimicrobial therapy for an average of 3 weeks is often curative even without surgery so long as:

  • The diagnosis has been well established

  • The patient has no evidence of joint space involvement

  • The patient has repsonded well to therapy within 48 hours

  • The patient has not failed a full course of treatment

For adults, surgical therapy is typically required followed by appropriate antimicrobial therapy, based on culture and sensitivity data.

Empiric treatment with vancomycin plus either cefepime or piperacillin-tazobactam is reasonable, pending these data.

Brodie’s Abscess

Epidemiology

  • A chronic localized bone abscess, usually singular.

  • Most often located in the distal tibia near the metaphysis.

  • Three quarters of patients are <25 years old.

Presentation

Subacute: patients usually present with fever, pain, and periosteal elevation.

Chronic: patients usually present with long-standing dull bone pain in the absence of fever.

Diagnosis

Labs: CBC, CMP, ESR, and CRP

Imaging: XR vs CT vs. MRI (gold standard)

Microbiology: aerobic, anaerobic, fungal, and AFB bone cultures with or without next generation sequencing

Pathology: bone biopsy

Management

Surgical therapy and pathogen-directed antibiotics are typical
